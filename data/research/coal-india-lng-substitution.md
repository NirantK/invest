This policy environment, which includes careful monitoring of gas price ceilings by the Petroleum Planning & Analysis Cell (PPAC), remains highly favorable for coal.

In India's power sector during the 2025-2026 period, there is a clear trend of substitution away from natural gas and towards thermal coal, driven by economic factors. Tight global Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) market conditions and elevated spot LNG benchmarks have significantly increased the variable cost of gas-fired power generation. This makes coal the more cost-effective fuel for baseload and mid-merit power dispatch. An analysis of fuel-wise generation data from the Central Electricity Authority (CEA) for the period of April 2025 to February 2026 confirms this increased reliance on thermal generation. Thermal generation was a primary contributor to this output, underscoring coal's role as the backbone of the power system in meeting rising demand. This dynamic, where merit-order dispatch favors cheaper coal over expensive imported gas, reinforces coal offtake and supports the volumes for producers like Coal India.

# Comparative Cost Analysis Coal Vs Lng

During the 2025-2026 period, domestic coal holds a significant cost-effectiveness advantage over imported Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) for power generation in India. The primary reason is the prevailing market condition of tight global LNG supply and consequently higher spot LNG price benchmarks for India. This directly translates to higher variable fuel costs for gas-fired power plants. As a result, under the merit-order dispatch principle used in the Indian power system, coal-based generation is consistently favored over gas-based generation for meeting baseload and mid-merit demand. Gas-fired plants are often pushed out of merit and operate at low plant load factors, being called upon only for specific grid balancing, localized needs, or where firm contractual obligations exist. The Indian government's close monitoring of gas price ceilings and LNG import costs, evidenced by trackers from the Petroleum Planning & Analysis Cell (PPAC), highlights the policy sensitivity to gas price volatility and reinforces the strategic reliance on more affordable domestic coal to ensure both energy affordability and national energy security.

# Investment Thesis Outlook And Validation

The LNG substitution thesis remains firmly intact, as elevated costs associated with LNG-linked gas continue to place gas-fired power plants largely out of the merit order for dispatch, except for specific contracted or localized grid-balancing needs. This dynamic preserves coal's substantial share in India's power generation mix and underpins demand for Coal India's output. Looking ahead through the remainder of 2026 and into FY27, Coal India's profitability is expected to remain robust, supported by significant e-auction volumes sold at considerable premiums over notified prices. While absolute auction prices may normalize from the peaks seen in prior years, the premium realization on large tonnages will continue to be a primary earnings lever. Investors should closely monitor quarterly disclosures on auction premiums, any shift in the sales mix towards higher-grade coal, and the blend between lower-priced linkage coal and higher-priced auction coal. To triangulate the strength of the substitution trend, key signals to watch include IEX power exchange volumes and market clearing prices (as indicators of supply-demand tightness), PPAC reports on LNG and domestic gas prices (to gauge gas competitiveness), and CEA's monthly generation and Plant Load Factor (PLF) trends (to assess thermal plant utilization). From a policy perspective, continued government focus on national production targets near or above 1 billion tonnes, efforts to de-bottleneck logistics, and any reforms to e-auction frameworks or price resets will be crucial. In conclusion, with LNG-linked gas expected to remain relatively expensive, India's power system will continue to lean heavily on coal to meet demand growth. This fundamental fuel-price dynamic is likely to keep the coal-versus-gas substitution thesis favorable for Coal India well into FY27, barring a sharp and sustained global decline in LNG prices.

# Potential Risks To Thesis

Several potential risks and countervailing factors could weaken or negate the Coal India LNG substitution thesis. The most significant risk is a sharp and sustained decline in global LNG prices. If gas price volatility eases and prices fall considerably, the variable cost of gas-fired power plants would decrease, improving their position in the merit-order dispatch and making them more competitive against coal, particularly during shoulder seasons with lower power demand. Another major risk stems from the energy transition. The rapid and ongoing addition of renewable energy capacity, particularly solar, can displace coal-fired generation, especially during daylight hours. Similarly, strong seasonality in hydropower generation, such as during a good monsoon, can transiently reduce the demand for thermal power, as hydro is a cheaper source. Although thermal power is expected to remain the balancing backbone of the grid in FY26, the increasing penetration of these alternative sources is a persistent headwind. Finally, changes in government policy present a risk. Any downward revision of domestic gas price caps or ceilings could improve the economics for gas power plants. Likewise, any regulatory reforms that negatively impact Coal India's e-auction frameworks or mandate significant changes to notified price resets could alter the company's profitability and the market dynamics that currently favor coal.
