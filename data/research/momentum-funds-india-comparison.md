# Index Methodology Comparison

A detailed comparison of the methodologies reveals key differences in stock selection and weighting, while rebalancing frequency is consistent.

**Stock Selection Criteria:**
- The Nifty 200 Momentum 30, Nifty 500 Momentum 50, and Nifty Midcap150 Momentum 50 indices employ a similar, pure momentum-based selection process. For each stock within their respective parent universes (Nifty 200, Nifty 500, Nifty Midcap 150), a 'Normalised Momentum Score' is calculated. This score is derived from the stock's 6-month and 12-month price returns, adjusted for volatility. The top-ranked stocks (30 for the N200 M30, and 50 for the N500 M50 and Midcap150 M50) based on this score are chosen for the index.
- In contrast, the Nifty Smallcap250 Momentum Quality 100 index uses a multi-factor approach. It selects its 100 constituents from the Nifty Smallcap 250 universe based on a combination of their momentum and quality factor scores. This addition of a quality screen is a significant differentiator, designed to mitigate some of the inherent risks associated with small-cap stocks.

**Weighting Mechanism:**
- The Nifty 200 Momentum 30 factsheet specifies that stock weights are based on a combination of the stock’s 'Normalised Momentum Score' and its free-float market capitalization. This dual-factor weighting gives higher allocation to stocks that not only have strong momentum but also larger market capitalizations.
- While not explicitly detailed for the Nifty 500 Momentum 50 and Nifty Midcap150 Momentum 50 in the provided text, they likely follow a similar weighting methodology, as this is a standard approach for Nifty's strategy indices.
- The specific weighting for the Nifty Smallcap250 Momentum Quality 100 is not described, but it would logically be derived from the combined momentum and quality scores used for selection, potentially also factoring in free-float market capitalization.

**Rebalancing Frequency:**
- All four indices are reviewed and reconstituted on a semi-annual basis. This ensures that the portfolios are regularly updated to reflect the stocks that currently exhibit the strongest characteristics according to each index's specific strategy.

# Analysis Of Nifty 200 Momentum 30

The Nifty 200 Momentum 30 index is designed to track the performance of 30 high-momentum stocks selected from its parent index, the Nifty 200. This gives it a distinct large-cap orientation, as the Nifty 200 comprises the 200 largest companies by free-float market capitalization. The selection methodology involves calculating a 'Normalised Momentum Score' for each stock based on its 6-month and 12-month price returns, adjusted for volatility. Stock weights are then determined by a combination of this momentum score and free-float market capitalization. This construction leads to a portfolio that is relatively stable compared to mid-cap or small-cap momentum strategies. According to its factsheet, it has a beta of 0.82 relative to the Nifty 50 and a standard deviation of 19.04. As of February 2026, its performance figures were approximately 21.5% (1-year), 18.1% (3-year), and 15.7% (5-year). Due to its foundation in a liquid, large-cap universe, it tends to experience milder drawdowns and faster recoveries during market corrections compared to more aggressive momentum indices. This makes it a suitable core momentum holding for long-term investors seeking robust risk-adjusted returns without excessive volatility.

# Analysis Of Nifty 500 Momentum 50

The Nifty 500 Momentum 50 index offers a broad approach to momentum investing by selecting the top 50 companies based on their 'Normalized Momentum Score' from the Nifty 500 universe. This parent index represents approximately 96% of the free-float market capitalization of the Indian stock market, encompassing large, mid, and small-cap segments. Consequently, the Nifty 500 Momentum 50 provides diversified market-cap exposure, capturing momentum wherever it appears across the spectrum. This makes it a potential core momentum holding for investors who prefer not to be concentrated in a single market-cap segment. Its risk profile, with a beta of 0.83 and a standard deviation of 20.71, places it between the more stable Nifty 200 Momentum 30 and the more volatile mid-cap focused indices. While its long-run performance has shown a similar momentum premium, short-term performance can vary. The context notes that in the 2025-2026 period, its short-term results were weaker compared to the N200 M30. It is described as a 'reasonable middle path' for investors seeking factor breadth and wider market inclusion in their momentum strategy.

# Analysis Of Nifty Midcap150 Momentum 50

The Nifty Midcap150 Momentum 50 index is specifically designed to capture the momentum factor within the mid-cap segment of the Indian market. It selects the top 50 companies from the Nifty Midcap 150 index based on their 'Normalized Momentum Score'. This focus on mid-caps offers higher growth potential compared to large-cap indices, as mid-sized companies are often in a high-growth phase. # Analysis Of Nifty Smallcap250 Momentum Quality 100

The Nifty Smallcap250 Momentum Quality 100 index employs a unique dual-factor strategy, targeting the volatile small-cap segment. It selects 100 stocks from the Nifty Smallcap 250 universe based on a combination of their momentum and quality factor scores. The 'quality' filter—which typically includes metrics like return on equity, financial leverage, and earnings stability—is intended to mitigate some of the inherent risks associated with small-cap investing. Despite this quality screen, the index maintains a high-risk, high-return profile and exhibits the highest cyclicality among the compared momentum indices. As of February 2026, its performance was approximately 17.0% (1-year), 16.7% (3-year), and 17.6% (5-year). Its risk metrics include a beta of 0.76 and a standard deviation of 19.69. # Momentum Investing Primer

Momentum investing is a factor-based investment strategy that aims to capitalize on the continuance of existing market trends. The core principle is that securities that have demonstrated strong performance in the recent past will continue to outperform, while those that have underperformed will continue to do so. The Nifty momentum indices operationalize this by calculating a 'Normalised Momentum Score' for each eligible company. According to the index methodology, this score is determined by the company's 6-month and 12-month price returns, adjusted for volatility. The top-ranking stocks based on this score are then included in the index. The indices are typically rebalanced semi-annually to remove stocks with waning momentum and add new leaders, ensuring the portfolio remains aligned with the momentum factor. This strategy is considered a potential source of excess returns because it systematically harvests the behavioral biases of investors, such as under-reacting to good news and chasing trends.

# Key Risks Of Momentum Investing

Investing in momentum strategies comes with a distinct set of risks that investors must consider:

1.  **Factor Cyclicality and Underperformance**: The momentum factor is cyclical and does not outperform in all market conditions. There can be prolonged periods where momentum strategies underperform the broader market, particularly during sharp market reversals when leadership rotates from growth/momentum stocks to value stocks.

2.  **Sharp Drawdowns ('Momentum Crashes')**: Momentum strategies are susceptible to sudden and severe declines, often referred to as 'momentum crashes'. These typically occur at market inflection points. The provided data confirms this risk, showing higher standard deviation and beta for momentum indices compared to broad market benchmarks. For instance, the Nifty Midcap150 Momentum 50 and Nifty Smallcap250 Momentum Quality 100 exhibit higher volatility, implying a greater potential for deep and prolonged drawdowns during market corrections.

3.  **Higher Portfolio Churn**: To maintain exposure to the strongest performing stocks, momentum indices are rebalanced periodically (semi-annually in the case of the Nifty indices). This results in a higher portfolio turnover rate compared to a passive buy-and-hold strategy. High churn can lead to increased transaction costs within the fund, which can be a drag on performance, and may also have tax implications.

4.  **Concentration Risk**: These indices are often concentrated in a limited number of stocks (e.g., 30 or 50) and may become heavily weighted towards specific sectors that are currently in favor, increasing sector-specific risk.

# Taxation Implications For Investors

For Indian investors, the gains from equity index funds (funds with over 65% of their portfolio in domestic equities) are subject to capital gains tax. The rules applicable as of 2026 are as follows:

*   **Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG)**: If an investor sells their fund units within 12 months from the date of purchase, the resulting profit is considered a Short-Term Capital Gain. STCG is taxed at a flat rate of 15%, plus applicable cess and surcharge.

*   **Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG)**: If the fund units are held for more than 12 months before being sold, the profit is classified as a Long-Term Capital Gain. For equity funds, LTCG is taxed at a rate of 10% (plus applicable cess and surcharge) on the portion of the gain that exceeds ₹1,00,000 in a single financial year. Long-term capital gains up to ₹1 lakh are exempt from tax.

It is important to note that the high portfolio churn inherent in momentum funds, due to their periodic rebalancing, can lead to the realization of capital gains within the fund itself. While these internal transactions do not create an immediate tax liability for the investor, they can affect the fund's Net Asset Value (NAV). The investor's tax liability only arises when they redeem their own units.
