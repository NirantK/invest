# Executive Summary

As of April 15, 2026, a significant number of international mutual funds in India are open for fresh subscriptions and accommodate monthly investments of at least ₹1 lakh per PAN. The availability of these funds is dynamic, heavily influenced by the overseas investment limits set by SEBI and the RBI. When an Asset Management Company (AMC) approaches its allocated limit, it may temporarily halt or cap new investments, while funds from AMCs with available headroom remain open. Key distribution platforms like Groww and Value Research list these available funds. Among the open schemes, investors have diverse options across several categories. For US Tech-focused exposure, notable funds include the passively managed Motilal Oswal Nasdaq-100 FoF and the actively managed Edelweiss US Technology Equity FoF. In the Broad Developed Markets category, the Franklin U.S. Opportunities Equity Active FoF is a prominent choice. For investors seeking Emerging Markets exposure, funds like the Nippon India Taiwan Equity Fund have shown extraordinary returns, albeit with higher risk. Other available options include thematic funds like the Axis Global Innovation FoF. While many funds are open without explicit caps below ₹1 lakh per month, investors should always verify the latest subscription status and any specific investment limits directly with the AMC or on their chosen investment platform before committing funds.

## Impact On Subscriptions

The regulatory caps set by SEBI and the RBI are absolute ceilings on the net overseas equity holdings for the mutual fund industry. When an individual Asset Management Company's (AMC) total exposure approaches its USD 1 billion limit (or USD 300 million for ETFs), it is mandated to stop accepting fresh inflows into its international schemes. To manage this, AMCs may either completely close the funds for new subscriptions or impose strict limits on investment amounts (e.g., per-PAN, per-day, or SIP caps) to preserve the remaining headroom. This regulatory mechanism is the primary reason for the dynamic subscription status of international funds, where some are listed as 'closed' or 'capped' while others with sufficient unused capacity remain open for new investments. SEBI permits AMCs to accept new investments up to the unused portion of the cap, which can lead to funds reopening or increasing their investment limits when headroom becomes available through market movements or redemptions.

## Analysis Summary

As of April 2026, open international funds can be grouped into several distinct categories, each with a unique risk-reward profile. 'US Tech-focused' funds offer high growth potential but come with significant concentration risk in the technology sector. They are available as both low-cost passive ETF Fund of Funds (FoFs) tracking indices like the Nasdaq-100, and as higher-cost active FoFs where managers select specific tech stocks. 'Broad Developed Markets' funds provide exposure to large, stable economies, primarily the US, through actively managed portfolios. 'Emerging Markets / Country-specific' funds present the highest risk and potential reward, with performance heavily tied to the economic and geopolitical situation of a single country or region (e.g., Taiwan, China, Japan); their returns can be exceptionally high but also highly volatile. Finally, 'Global Diversified / Innovation Thematic' funds spread risk across multiple regions and sectors, focusing on long-term trends like technological innovation, offering a balance between diversification and thematic growth.

# Investment Strategy And Risk Insights

## Strategy Type

Multiple Strategies: Passive, Active, Thematic, and Country-Specific

## Risk Reward Profile

Different international fund strategies offer varied risk-reward profiles. Passive ETF FoFs provide market-linked returns at a very low cost, but their performance is entirely dependent on the underlying index, carrying concentration risk if the index is not diversified. Active FoFs have higher expense ratios but offer the potential for alpha generation through skilled manager selection of stocks or underlying funds. Country-specific funds carry the highest idiosyncratic risk, including geopolitical and currency risks, but can deliver spectacular returns when the target country's market performs exceptionally well. Global Thematic FoFs offer a middle ground, providing diversification across regions while concentrating on a specific high-growth theme like innovation, which spreads risk but still focuses on a particular investment thesis.

## Portfolio Concentration Details

Portfolio concentration varies significantly by strategy. Passive ETF FoFs, such as those tracking the Nasdaq-100, have high concentration in a single index, which can lead to heavy weighting in a few large-cap tech stocks. Active FoFs have concentration determined by the fund manager's mandate and conviction, which often results in a portfolio that is heavy in specific sectors like technology for US-focused funds. Country-specific funds exhibit the most extreme concentration, with risk heavily weighted towards a single geography and often a few dominant sectors within it, such as the semiconductor industry in Taiwan. Global Thematic funds are concentrated in stocks related to a specific theme (e.g., AI, clean energy) but are typically diversified across multiple countries.

# Key Definitions

## Term

SEBI/RBI Overseas Investment Limits

## Definition

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) and the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) have set caps on the total amount Indian mutual funds can invest in overseas securities. As of April 2026, the industry-wide limit for overseas equities is USD 7 billion, with an additional USD 1 billion allocated for investments in overseas Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs). Within this, each individual Asset Management Company (AMC) has a sub-limit of USD 1 billion for equities and USD 300 million for ETFs. When an AMC's total overseas investments approach its limit, it must stop accepting new money into its international funds to avoid breaching the cap. This is why some international funds are temporarily 'closed' or 'capped' (i.e., they limit the amount of new investment per person). AMCs can reopen subscriptions when redemptions or market movements create fresh headroom below the regulatory ceiling.
